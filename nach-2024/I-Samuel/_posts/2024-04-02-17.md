- Here we start to see David gain his agency and his identity. In particular, I think we can even start to see the roots of David’s role in Jewish liturgy as a stand-in for the Jewish people as a whole.
- This comes from the symbolism of the players in this story. Goliath is a stand-in for the enemies of *Yisrael*: physically stronger and better equipped. On the other hand, David is a stand-in for *Yisrael* itself: formidable not because of their physical strength, but because of their connection to God.
- I feel like this is driven home by the fact that Goliath is never referred to by name after his introduction, always just as “the Philistine.” In this way, his particular identity doesn’t matter: he is truly just representative of the enemy.

- *Honorable Mention:* I was curious about the etymology of Goliath’s name, since it wasn’t immediately obvious to me, and turns out there is archeological evidence that suggests that the name “Goliath” is a genuine Philistine name of the era! Thus why it has no obvious Hebrew etymology.
