{% vtr And David said to his heart, “Now, ... %}
{% endverses I Samuel 27:1 %}

- It is extremely refreshing to get some internal dialog! Not only are these early biblical stories in general focused less on what is happening in the minds of the characters, but as Alter points out, this is the first time we have had any insight into David’s thought process.
- I find the phrase which is used fascinating. Although there is of course a word for “thought” in Hebrew, I feel like most often it is just used to indicate the ideas which a character knows or is thinking about, rather than the specific words or thought processes they are thinking to themselves. In the Torah, I feel like this kind of internal dialog is usually just written as if it is said aloud, but here David is said to be specifically speaking to his heart.

<!--more-->
- This is also a good time to mention (which I may have already said on this blog) that in Biblical Hebrew the heart is primarily considered the organ from which *thought* comes, not the organ from which *emotions* come like in western culture. Thus, this could perhaps be more accurately translated as “David said to his mind” – or to break down the idiom even more – “David said in his mind.”
