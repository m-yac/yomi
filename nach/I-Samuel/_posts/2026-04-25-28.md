- After hearing repeated injunctions in the Torah like:
{% verses %}
{% vhe וְהַנֶּ֗פֶשׁ אֲשֶׁ֨ר תִּפְנֶ֤ה אֶל־הָֽאֹבֹת֙ וְאֶל־הַיִּדְּעֹנִ֔ים לִזְנֹ֖ת אַחֲרֵיהֶ֑ם וְנָתַתִּ֤י אֶת־פָּנַי֙ בַּנֶּ֣פֶשׁ הַהִ֔וא וְהִכְרַתִּ֥י אֹת֖וֹ מִקֶּ֥רֶב עַמּֽוֹ׃ %}
{% vtr And [as for] the soul that turns to the ghosts and the familiar spirits, going astray after them – I will set My face against that soul and I will cut them off from the midst of their people. %}
{% endverses Leviticus 20:6 %}
- I am thrilled to finally encounter an {{ 'אוב' | he: 'ov', 'ghost' }} or {{ 'ידעני' | he: 'yid‘oni', 'familiar spirit' }}! And what an encounter at that, I can’t believe this is in the *Tanakh*! At first it seemed strange to me that this matches so many of the modern tropes of summoning a ghost, but it makes sense since this scene is probably where all those tropes got started!
- I know that spirits existed as a part of Judaism for thousands of years in a quasi-accepted state, but they have been essentially entirely expunged from modern Judaism – far beyond the level they were at when the Torah was compiled, since even then the authors seemed to acknowledge their existence, just viewed interacting with them as forbidden.
- Perhaps the only relic of this I can think of in modern Judaism is the prophet Elijah, whose wandering spirit visits circumcisions and Passover seders.
