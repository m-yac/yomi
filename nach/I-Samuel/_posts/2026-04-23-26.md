- I think something finally clicked over what stories like this are trying to teach. A bunch of times in the tanakh, most memorable to me the hardening of Pharaoh’s heart in the Exodus story, the actions of God seem to (on a literary level) undermine the achievements or agency of the characters.
- For example, in this story I found myself slightly miffed – like I was so impressed by David’s shrewdness and cunning, and right at the end we find out that if it wasn’t for God putting everyone in a deep sleep, they probably would’ve woken up? It made me think, maybe David isn’t so smart after all, if God hadn’t intervened, his smart plan would have almost certainly resulted in him walking right into his own death!

<!--more-->
- But that frustration went away when I think it finally clicked what these story elements are saying. In this case, it is absolutely undermining David’s agency and impressiveness, because it’s making the point that it isn’t because of David that this happened, it *is* because of God.
- The lesson (or perhaps just *a* lesson) of this story is that in the end it is only through God, and God’s favor, that miraculous things occur. From that perspective, David’s confidence that Saul or his soldiers won’t wake up at his presence, although not discussed, one can infer was either placed in his heart by God, or was due to his faith. And the ability to trust that and not have doubt is an achievement – one that I really struggle with.
- In the end, I’m actually quite grateful that the takeaway of this story for me is not ‘well, David is just a really good military leader’ and instead is that through God and faith in God incredible things like this are possible. It touches me personally, rather than making me just be like, wow this guy is cool, hunh?
